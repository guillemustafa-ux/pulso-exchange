# Stablecoins and the dollar in Argentina

A stablecoin is a crypto token designed to hold a stable value, usually pegged 1-to-1 to the US dollar. The two most used are **USDT** (Tether) and **USDC** (Circle): both claim to hold equivalent reserves in dollars and liquid assets for every token issued, and publish periodic reserve reports, though with different levels of transparency and auditing between them. There are also algorithmic stablecoins, which try to hold the peg with market mechanisms instead of reserves — the most remembered example is the collapse of **UST/Luna in May 2022**, which lost its dollar parity within days and wiped out tens of billions of dollars, a central lesson on why the design of the backing matters as much as the "stablecoin" label.

In Argentina, stablecoins played a very concrete role: with currency controls (the "cepo cambiario") limiting access to the official dollar and a historic gap between the official rate, the MEP rate, the CCL rate and the informal "blue" dollar, buying USDT became, for many people, the simplest way to hold dollars without going through the traditional banking system and its operational limits. This explains why P2P trading volume in USDT in Argentina ranks among the highest in the region: it isn't speculation, it's a direct response to the need to save in a currency that doesn't lose purchasing power to peso inflation.

But "stable" doesn't mean "risk-free". Even USDC, considered one of the most solid, briefly lost its peg in March 2023 when part of its reserves got trapped in the collapse of Silicon Valley Bank — it returned to 1:1 within days, but it showed that counterparty risk (where and how the reserves are held) is real even for the largest stablecoins. Buying USDT or USDC on an unregulated exchange, or holding them in a wallet without basic security precautions, adds another layer of risk that has nothing to do with the coin itself but with where you keep it.
